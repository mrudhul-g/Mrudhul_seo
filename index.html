Why Sublimation for Cricket

Why Sublimation is the Best Choice for Cricket Kits

Cricket is a sport of long durations — a full day match, a 50-over game, or even a T20 played in the afternoon heat of Kerala demands a fabric that performs. Traditional screen-printed or iron-on transfer jerseys simply cannot keep up. Sublimation printing on high-quality polyester solves all these problems.

The Sublimation Advantage for Cricket

Sublimation dye bonds directly with polyester fibres at a molecular level. This means there is no layer of ink sitting on top of the fabric — the print IS the fabric. The result is a jersey that breathes as well as an unprinted blank. It stays cool, wicks moisture away from the body, and maintains its vibrant colours through dozens of washes without fading.

Compare this to screen printing, where ink layers can feel heavy and can crack after repeated washing and use — not ideal for active sportswear used season after season.

The Technology

How Sublimation Printing Works

Sublimation printing is a process where solid dye is converted directly into gas (bypassing the liquid phase) using heat and pressure. This gas penetrates the surface of polyester fabric or hard substrates like ceramic mugs, bonding at a molecular level with the material.

The Process Step by Step

  • Design: Your artwork is printed in mirror-reverse onto special sublimation transfer paper using Epson UltraChrome DS inks.
  • Transfer: The printed paper is placed face-down on the substrate (jersey, mug, etc.) and passed through a heat press at 180–210°C.
  • Sublimation: The heat converts the ink to gas, which is absorbed into the polyester fibres or coated substrate surface.
  • Result: A permanent, full-colour, photo-quality image that cannot be peeled, cracked, or washed off.

About Screen Printing

What is Screen Printing and How Does It Work?

Screen printing is one of the oldest and most reliable printing methods in the world — and for good reason. A mesh screen is prepared with a stencil of your design, and ink is pushed through the open areas of the mesh directly onto the fabric using a squeegee. Each colour requires a separate screen.

Why Screen Printing is Ideal for Bulk Orders

The setup cost for screen printing (making the screens) is a fixed one-time expense per order. This means the per-unit cost drops significantly as quantity increases — making screen printing highly cost-effective for bulk orders of 50 pieces or more.

The inks used in screen printing are also extremely durable — plastisol and water-based inks bond tightly with cotton fabric and maintain their colour through hundreds of washes. For simple, bold designs with 1–4 spot colours on cotton t-shirts, screen printing delivers outstanding results.

Types of Screen Printing We Offer

  • Spot Colour Printing: Each colour is printed separately — best for logos, text, and designs with clean, defined colours. Most common type.
  • Halftone Printing: Uses dots of varying size to simulate gradients and photographic images using a limited number of inks.
  • Discharge Printing: Removes the dye from the fabric in the print area, creating a soft-hand print that feels like part of the shirt rather than sitting on top of it.
  • Puff Printing: Special ink that rises when heated, creating a raised 3D effect — popular for logos and branded merchandise.

About DTG Printing

What is Digital (DTG) T-Shirt Printing?

DTG stands for Direct-to-Garment — a digital printing method where a specialised inkjet printer applies water-based inks directly onto the surface of a t-shirt, much like how a regular inkjet printer prints on paper. The result is a full-colour, photo-quality print with virtually unlimited colour capability and no screens, stencils, or setup fees.

Key Advantages of Digital Printing

  • No minimum order: Print as little as 1 piece — perfect for gifts, prototypes, and samples.
  • Unlimited colours: Gradients, photos, complex artwork — all printed in one pass without any colour limitations.
  • Photo-quality output: Print family photos, product images, and intricate artwork with stunning detail.
  • Quick turnaround: No screen setup means orders can be ready in 1–2 days for small quantities.
  • Eco-friendly: Water-based inks and no screen chemicals make DTG a cleaner printing option.

When to Choose Digital Printing

Digital printing is the best choice when your design has more than 4 colours, includes photographs or gradients, or when you need a small quantity quickly. It is also ideal when you need individual personalisation — for example, 30 t-shirts each with a different name or photo.

For cotton t-shirts in smaller quantities, digital printing is an excellent option with no setup cost and no minimum order. For larger bulk quantities with simpler designs, screen printing is worth considering. For sportswear and polyester, sublimation is the recommended method.

Digital Printing on Dark vs Light Fabrics

Digital printing works excellently on white and light-coloured cotton t-shirts. On dark fabrics, a white underbase layer is applied first, which still delivers excellent results. The fabric must be pre-treated to ensure the ink adheres properly.

Screen Printing vs Sublimation Printing:
Which Should You Choose for Jerseys?

By Mrudhul Custom Prints · Ernakulam, Kochi · 8 min read

If you're ordering custom jerseys or t-shirts for your team, club, or event in Kerala, one of the first decisions you'll face is choosing a printing method. Screen printing and sublimation printing are the two most popular options — but they work very differently and suit different needs. This guide breaks it all down so you can make the right choice.

What is Screen Printing?

Screen printing is a traditional method where ink is pushed through a fine mesh screen (one screen per colour) directly onto the fabric. It has been used for decades to print t-shirts, uniforms, and promotional apparel. The result is a solid, opaque print that sits on top of the fabric — vibrant, bold, and extremely durable on cotton.

Screen printing works best on cotton or cotton-blend fabrics. Each colour in your design requires a separate screen to be prepared, which means there is a setup cost involved. However, once the screens are ready, printing each piece is fast and cheap — making screen printing highly economical for orders of 50 pieces or more with simple, bold designs.

Best for: Large bulk orders (100+ pieces), designs with 1–4 solid colours, cotton t-shirts for events, NGO campaigns, factory uniforms, and political campaigns.

What is Sublimation Printing?

Sublimation printing is a newer, digital process where heat converts solid dye into gas, which bonds permanently into the fibres of polyester fabric. The result is an all-over, seamless print that cannot peel, crack, or fade — because the dye literally becomes part of the fabric rather than sitting on top of it.

Sublimation requires 100% polyester fabric to work. On cotton, the dye doesn't bond and the result is a washed-out, faded image. However, on polyester sportswear, sublimation produces results that screen printing simply cannot match — full-colour, all-over, photo-quality designs with no white background edges or colour limitations.

Best for: Football jerseys, cricket kits, basketball uniforms, all-over designs, sportswear, and any application requiring moisture-wicking polyester fabric.

What Affects the Price — Key Factors

Understanding what drives the cost helps you make smarter decisions about your order before you call.

1. Quantity

This is the single biggest factor. Printing 200 pieces costs significantly less per unit than printing 20 pieces. Fixed costs (screen preparation, machine setup, ink preparation) are spread across more units in larger orders, dramatically reducing the per-piece price.

2. Printing Method

Screen printing is cheapest for large bulk orders with simple designs. Digital printing is more expensive per piece but has no setup cost — making it better for small quantities or complex designs. Sublimation is priced in the middle but is the only option for polyester sportswear.

3. Number of Colours

In screen printing, each colour requires a separate screen. A 4-colour design will cost more than a 1-colour design. In digital and sublimation printing, the number of colours doesn't affect price.

4. Print Size and Placement

A full front + full back print costs more than a left chest logo only. Additional print locations (sleeves, collar, hem) each add to the cost. Sublimation all-over printing is priced as a complete garment regardless of print area.

5. Fabric and Garment Quality

The blank garment itself is a significant cost variable. Heavier GSM fabrics cost more than lighter ones, and polyester performance jerseys are priced differently from basic cotton tees. We stock multiple options — call us to discuss what fits your budget and quality requirements.

How Bulk Discounts Work in Kerala Print Shops

Designing a custom football jersey can feel overwhelming if you haven't done it before. Between colours, placement, fonts, file formats, and fabric choices — there are many decisions to make. This guide walks you through the entire process, whether you're designing for a school team, a local club, or a corporate football league in Kochi.

Step 1: Choose Your Team Colours

Start with your primary and secondary colours. Most football jerseys use two to three colours — a dominant base colour, a secondary colour for panels or stripes, and sometimes an accent for text and numbers.

Key considerations:

  • Contrast: Your jersey colours should contrast strongly with your shorts/trousers. A dark blue jersey with dark blue shorts is hard to distinguish on the field.
  • Opponent colours: If you're in a league, check what colours common opponents wear to avoid clashing. Many teams have a home and away kit for this reason.
  • Brand colours: If you have a club crest or sponsor with specific colours, build the jersey palette around those to maintain visual identity.

For sublimation printing (the method we use for football jerseys), you can use any colour — there are no colour limitations. Gradients, ombre effects, and complex patterns are all achievable.

Step 2: Plan Your Jersey Layout

Think about the overall visual structure of the jersey — not just the logo placement. Consider:

  • Panels and colour blocking: Side panels in a contrasting colour, raglan sleeves, a yoke panel across the shoulders — these structural elements define the jersey's silhouette.
  • Stripes and patterns: Vertical stripes, horizontal chest stripes, chevron patterns, geometric designs — all work beautifully with sublimation's all-over capability.
  • Collar and sleeve design: The collar and sleeve cuffs are important design real estate. A contrasting ribbed collar or a striped cuff adds polish.

Sketch a rough front and back layout on paper first, or use a free tool like Canva or Adobe Express to mock it up digitally before finalising.

Step 3: Add Team Name, Logo, Numbers and Sponsors

Team Name and Crest

The team name or club crest typically goes on the upper left chest (from the viewer's perspective). If you have an official club badge, use a high-resolution version (vector AI/EPS file is best). We can print text as a substitute for a crest if your club doesn't have one.

Player Numbers

Squad numbers go on the back, centred, in the upper-middle area of the jersey. Standard sizes are 20–25cm in height for the number. Choose a font that is bold and readable — thin decorative fonts are hard to read from a distance on the pitch.

Player Names

Player names go above the number on the back, in a smaller font (typically 5–7cm height). Use all-caps for readability. For personalised orders, provide us with a spreadsheet listing jersey number → player name → size for each player.

Sponsor Logos

Main sponsor logo goes on the chest (centre front), below the team name. Secondary sponsors can go on sleeves, the back below the number, or the collar. Send sponsor logo files in vector format (AI, EPS) or high-resolution PNG with transparent background.

Step 4: Choose Your Fabric

For football jerseys printed with sublimation, the fabric must be 100% polyester. Within polyester, there are options:

  • Standard dry-fit polyester (140–160 GSM): Lightweight, highly breathable, excellent for hot Kerala weather. Ideal for most club and school teams.
  • Premium performance polyester (180–200 GSM): Slightly heavier, better structure, preferred by serious clubs and professional teams. More durable for rough contact sport.
  • Mesh polyester: Maximum breathability with a textured feel. Popular for warm-weather tournaments.

Best Fabrics for Custom Jersey & T-Shirt Printing: Complete Guide

By Mrudhul Custom Prints · Ernakulam, Kochi · 7 min read

Polyester vs Cotton vs Dri-Fit vs Blends — which fabric is right for your printing need? Plus a GSM guide for making the right quality decision.

The fabric you choose for your custom t-shirts or jerseys affects not just how they look — but how long they last, how comfortable they feel to wear, and critically, which printing methods will work on them. This guide covers the four main fabric types used for custom printing in Kerala and when to use each.

100% Cotton

Cotton is the most popular fabric for everyday t-shirts, event tees, uniforms, and casual wear. It is soft against the skin, breathable in Kerala's humid climate, and easy to care for. Cotton absorbs moisture but doesn't wick it away — meaning it can feel heavy and clammy during intense physical activity.

Best printing methods: Screen printing, Digital (DTG) printing. Cotton does NOT work with sublimation — the dye doesn't bond with cotton fibres.

Best for: College fest tees, NGO campaign shirts, corporate event tees, restaurant uniforms, casual everyday wear, and any situation where comfort and print quality on a budget matter more than athletic performance.

Call us to discuss which cotton weight suits your project — we'll recommend the right GSM for your use case and budget.

100% Polyester

Polyester is the standard fabric for sportswear and performance clothing. It is lightweight, durable, quick-drying, and excellent at wicking moisture away from the body — essential for athletes playing in Kerala's heat and humidity. Polyester is the only fabric that works with sublimation printing, making it the go-to for custom jerseys.

Best printing methods: Sublimation printing only. Screen printing on polyester can cause dye migration (the fabric dye bleeds into the ink). Digital printing also has limitations on polyester.

Best for: Football jerseys, cricket kits, basketball uniforms, volleyball jerseys, running vests, and all sportswear where moisture management and print durability matter.

Call us to discuss which jersey spec suits your sport and budget — we stock multiple GSM options and can show you samples.

Cotton-Polyester Blend (60:40 or 65:35)

Blended fabrics combine the comfort of cotton with some of the performance properties of polyester. They are softer than pure polyester but more breathable and somewhat moisture-wicking compared to pure cotton. A popular middle-ground choice.

Best printing methods: Screen printing and digital printing work well. Sublimation works partially on blends with 65%+ polyester — colours will appear around 30–40% less vibrant than on 100% polyester. Not recommended for sublimation if colour accuracy matters.

Best for: Corporate polo shirts, casual team tees, merchandise where both comfort and some performance are needed.

Dri-Fit / Performance Fabric

Dri-Fit is Nike's brand name for moisture-wicking polyester — but the term is widely used to describe any high-performance moisture-management fabric. These are typically 100% polyester with a special weave or treatment that dramatically accelerates sweat evaporation.

Best printing methods: Sublimation printing. The polyester base makes dri-fit fabrics ideal sublimation substrates.

Understanding GSM — What It Means for Your T-Shirt

GSM stands for Grams per Square Metre — it measures the weight and thickness of fabric. Higher GSM means heavier, thicker, and generally more premium-feeling fabric.

DTF Printing Explained: The Future of Custom T-Shirt Printing in India

By Mrudhul Custom Prints · Ernakulam, Kochi · 6 min read

What is Direct-to-Film (DTF) printing, how it works, and why it's rapidly becoming the most versatile method for custom apparel in India.

If you've been researching custom t-shirt printing in Kerala recently, you may have come across the term DTF printing. It is one of the newest and fastest-growing printing technologies in the Indian market — and for good reason. DTF (Direct-to-Film) printing solves several key limitations of older printing methods. Here is everything you need to know.

What is DTF Printing?

DTF stands for Direct-to-Film. Unlike DTG (Direct-to-Garment) which prints directly onto fabric, DTF printing works in two stages:

  1. Print on film: Your design is printed onto a special PET film using a DTF printer and CMYK + White inks.
  2. Transfer to fabric: Hot-melt adhesive powder is applied to the wet ink on the film, then heat-cured. The film is then heat-pressed onto the fabric, transferring the design permanently.

The result is a full-colour, highly detailed transfer that adheres to virtually any fabric — cotton, polyester, blends, denim, leather, nylon, and more.

Key Advantages of DTF Printing

  • Works on any fabric: Unlike sublimation (polyester only) or DTG (cotton best), DTF works on cotton, polyester, blends, and virtually every other textile.
  • No minimum order: Print a single custom transfer as easily as printing 100 units — no screen setup cost.
  • Full colour, no limitations: Gradients, photographs, and complex artwork all print with excellent quality.
  • Durable and wash-resistant: DTF transfers, when applied correctly, are flexible and highly wash-resistant — they stretch with the fabric without cracking.
  • Vibrant on dark fabrics: DTF prints vibrantly on black and dark fabrics without an underbase step — unlike DTG which requires pre-treatment for dark fabrics.
  • Fast turnaround: No screen preparation or pre-treatment means orders can be turned around very quickly.

Why DTF is Growing Rapidly in India

DTF fills a gap that no single existing technology could fill: full-colour, no-minimum printing on any fabric type. Previously, a customer wanting a photographic print on a dark polyester-cotton blend had no great option — sublimation wouldn't work well on the blend, DTG struggled on polyester, and screen printing couldn't do full colour cost-effectively at low quantities.

5 T-Shirt Printing Methods Explained: Screen, Digital, Sublimation, DTF & Heat Transfer

By Mrudhul Custom Prints · Ernakulam, Kochi · 9 min read

A complete, jargon-free breakdown of every major custom t-shirt printing method — with a quick decision guide to find the right one for your order.

Walk into any print shop in Kochi and mention "custom t-shirts" — and you'll be asked which printing method you want. If you don't know the difference between sublimation and DTG, the answer can be confusing. This guide demystifies all five major printing methods so you can make the right choice for your specific order.

Method 1: Screen Printing

Screen printing uses a mesh screen with a stencil of your design, through which ink is pushed onto the fabric. Each colour requires a separate screen. It is one of the oldest printing methods — and still the most economical for large bulk orders with simple, bold designs.

Pros: Very durable, economical at high quantities, vibrant opaque colours, works great on cotton, excellent for dark fabrics with underbase.

Cons: Setup cost per colour (screens), not ideal for gradients or photos, minimum order recommended (50+ pieces), not suitable for polyester/sportswear.

Best for: Event tees, NGO campaign shirts, restaurant uniforms, bulk corporate t-shirts (100+ pieces), political campaign shirts.

Method 2: Digital / DTG Printing

DTG (Direct-to-Garment) printing is like having an inkjet printer for t-shirts. A specialised printer jets water-based inks directly onto the fabric surface. No screens, no setup cost, unlimited colours, and no minimum order.

Pros: No minimum order, unlimited colours, photo-quality output, quick turnaround for small orders, no setup cost.

Cons: Works best on 100% cotton, not ideal for polyester, per-piece cost is higher than screen printing at large quantities, dark fabric printing requires pre-treatment.

Best for: Personalised gifts (1–10 pieces), complex full-colour designs in small quantities, photo t-shirts, prototype printing before bulk orders.

Method 3: Sublimation Printing

Sublimation converts solid dye to gas using heat, bonding it permanently into polyester fibres. The result is an all-over, seamless, full-colour print that is literally part of the fabric — it cannot peel, crack, or fade.

Pros: Permanent, never fades or peels, full all-over print capability, moisture-wicking polyester fabric ideal for sport, unlimited colours, excellent durability.

Cons: Only works on 100% polyester (or high-polyester blends), not suitable for cotton, only works on white or light-coloured fabric (can't print on dark polyester).

Best for: Football jerseys, cricket kits, basketball uniforms, sportswear of all kinds, photo mugs, cushion covers, and any all-over print application on polyester.

Method 4: DTF (Direct-to-Film) Printing

DTF prints your design onto a special PET film, then transfers it to fabric using heat press and adhesive powder. It works on virtually any fabric type — the most versatile modern printing method.

Pros: Works on any fabric (cotton, polyester, blends, denim, nylon), full colour, no minimum, excellent on dark fabrics, durable and flexible print.

Cons: Not suitable for all-over prints (placed transfers only), per-piece cost is higher than screen printing at large quantities, slightly raised texture compared to sublimation.

Best for: Full-colour prints on dark fabric, printing on cotton-polyester blends, small batches of complex designs, situations where fabric type is mixed across an order.

Method 5: Heat Transfer Vinyl (HTV)

HTV involves cutting designs from coloured vinyl sheets using a precision cutting machine, then heat-pressing the cut vinyl onto fabric. It produces a clean, professional look — particularly for names, numbers, and simple logos.

Pros: Excellent for names and numbers, clean crisp edges, works on most fabrics, no minimum order, relatively quick turnaround.

Cons: Not suitable for photographic or gradient designs, each colour is a separate layer of vinyl, can feel slightly stiff, not ideal for very large print areas.

Best for: Sports jersey numbers and names, simple logo uniforms, small quantity personalisation, adding individual names to pre-made jerseys.
